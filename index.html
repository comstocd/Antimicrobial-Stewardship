Antibiotic Stewardship for Internal Medicine Residents

A Practical Guide to Optimizing Antimicrobial Use

Evidence-Based Lecture

Why Stewardship Matters

The Scope of the Problem

  • Approximately 30% of antibiotic prescriptions are unnecessary or suboptimal (CDC estimate for outpatient settings)
  • In the United States (CDC 2023):
    • More than 2.8 million antimicrobial-resistant infections annually
    • Over 35,000 deaths per year
    • 143,000 emergency department visits for antibiotic-related adverse events
  • Globally: Over 4 million deaths in 2019 associated with AMR (with modeling uncertainties)
  • In hospitalized patients: 20% experience antibiotic-related adverse events

Consequences of Inappropriate Use

Immediate and Tangible Harms

  • Patient harm: C. difficile infections, allergic reactions, drug toxicity, organ damage
  • Clinical failures: Wrong drug, dose, or duration leading to treatment failures
  • Hospital readmissions: Complications from inappropriate therapy
  • Increased length of stay: With all associated risks
  • Antimicrobial resistance: A complex, evolving threat

Understanding the Resistance Landscape

U.S. Trends

Carbapenem-resistant Enterobacterales (CRE):
  • Common gut bacteria (E. coli, Klebsiella) resistant to last-resort carbapenems
  • NDM (New Delhi metallo-beta-lactamase): enzyme that destroys nearly all beta-lactams
  • Spreads on plasmids—small circular DNA pieces bacteria share
  • NDM-producing CRE increased 460% in U.S. (2019-2023)
  • Mortality: 30-50% for bloodstream infections
  • Candida auris: Nearly quadrupled (2019-2022)
  • COVID-19 impact: 20% increase in hospital-onset resistant infections (reflects system stress)

Core Principles of Stewardship

Three Primary Goals

  1. Optimize antibiotic use: Right drug, right dose, right duration
  2. Minimize adverse effects: From C. difficile to drug toxicity to resistance
  3. Prevent and reduce resistance: Preserve our antibiotic armamentarium

The Multidisciplinary Approach

  • Infectious diseases consultants: Clinical expertise, protocol development
  • Clinical pharmacists: Dosing optimization, therapeutic drug monitoring, IV-to-oral transitions
  • Microbiology laboratory staff: Diagnostic stewardship, stratified antibiograms
  • Nurses: Proper culture collection, antibiotic reviews
  • YOU - the prescribing physician: Daily decisions at point of care

Practical Stewardship Strategies

Key Interventions

  • Prospective Audit and Feedback: Expert review within 24-48 hours; reduces unnecessary antibiotic days by 37%
  • Preauthorization: Expert approval for broad-spectrum or last-resort antibiotics
  • Guidelines and Pathways: Evidence-based protocols tailored to local resistance
  • Pharmacy-Driven Interventions:
    • IV-to-oral conversion
    • Automatic stop orders (72-hour time-outs)
    • Duplicate therapy alerts
    • Dose optimization
  • Microbiology Support: Stratified antibiograms, selective reporting, rapid diagnostics

The Four Moments Framework

Moment 1: Does this patient need antibiotics?

  • Are there signs of bacterial infection beyond colonization?
  • Could this be viral, non-infectious, or self-limited?
  • Remember: ~30% of prescriptions are unnecessary or suboptimal

Moment 2: What diagnostics should I obtain?

  • Culture before antibiotics when safely possible
  • Order the right tests—not every fever needs blood cultures
  • Don't send urine cultures on asymptomatic patients

The Four Moments Framework (cont.)

Moment 3: What is optimal therapy?

  • Empiric: Use local guidelines and antibiograms
  • Definitive: De-escalate based on culture results
  • Choose narrow-spectrum agents when possible

Moment 4: When can I stop or narrow?

  • Daily reassessment: Are antibiotics still needed?
  • Can I narrow based on culture data?
  • Can I switch from IV to oral?
  • What is the appropriate total duration?

Critical Reminder

Unnecessarily prolonged antibiotic courses lead to patient harm and should be avoided.

High-Yield Scenarios: Sepsis

Key Point: Stewardship programs improve sepsis outcomes - they don't hinder care

Your Approach

  • Initiate prompt empiric therapy based on local antibiograms and suspected source
  • Implement protocols for rapid administration (within 1 hour)
  • Obtain cultures before antibiotics when possible, but don't delay treatment
  • Reassess daily: Tailor or stop therapy early when appropriate
  • De-escalate as soon as culture data and clinical response allow
  • Optimize duration based on source control and clinical improvement

Key principle: Fast initiation doesn't mean prolonged broad-spectrum therapy

High-Yield Scenarios: Skin Infections

Evidence-Based Approach

Targeted interventions reduced antibiotic duration by 3 days and broad-spectrum use by 30%—with no increase in clinical failures

Your Approach

  • Purulent infections (abscesses, furuncles):
    • I&D is primary therapy
    • Consider MRSA coverage initially, de-escalate based on cultures
  • Non-purulent cellulitis:
    • Often don't need MRSA coverage unless risk factors present:
    • Risk factors: Recent MRSA infection, injection drug use, recent incarceration, contact sports, HIV infection, recent hospitalization
    • Beta-lactam (cephalexin, cefazolin) usually sufficient for uncomplicated cases
  • Duration: 5 days if improving for uncomplicated infections

High-Yield Scenarios: Asymptomatic Bacteriuria

The Problem: We dramatically overtreat ASB

Evidence

Stewardship interventions reduced ASB treatment, with antibiotic use decreasing by 30% over 30 months - sustained without patient harm

Your Approach

  • Only obtain urine cultures in patients with urinary symptoms
  • Pyuria alone is NOT an indication for treatment
  • Positive cultures in catheterized patients usually represent colonization
  • Only treat ASB in two populations:
    • Pregnant patients
    • Before urologic procedures with expected bleeding

High-Yield Scenarios: Community-Acquired Pneumonia

Evidence

Stewardship improved appropriate CAP treatment from 65% to 91%. Guideline-adherent therapy significantly reduces mortality.

Your Approach

  • Determine if bacterial:
    • Use respiratory viral panels to rule out viral etiologies
    • Procalcitonin (PCT) can help assess bacterial likelihood (IDSA/ATS: don't use PCT alone to initiate therapy)
    • Serial PCT: Can support early antibiotic discontinuation when declining (greater than or equal to 80% from peak or less than 0.5 ng/mL) plus clinical improvement
  • Empiric therapy:
    • Beta-lactam plus macrolide OR respiratory fluoroquinolone for most
    • Add MRSA coverage only with risk factors (test MRSA colonization)
    • Add Pseudomonal coverage only with structural lung disease or recent healthcare exposure
  • Obtain respiratory cultures when feasible to tailor therapy
  • Duration: 5-7 days for most patients who improve (serial PCT can guide early discontinuation)
  • Switch to oral within 2-3 days when appropriate

High-Yield Scenarios: Urinary Tract Infections

Your Approach

  • Distinguish: Cystitis vs. pyelonephritis vs. asymptomatic bacteriuria
  • Only obtain urine cultures in patients with urinary symptoms
  • Don't treat ASB except in pregnancy or before urologic procedures with bleeding
  • Uncomplicated cystitis: 3 days for most agents (nitrofurantoin, TMP-SMX)
  • Avoid fluoroquinolones for uncomplicated UTI when alternatives exist
  • Pyelonephritis: 5-7 days with pathogen-directed therapy if clinical improvement

Key Point: Pyuria alone is NOT an indication for treatment

Diagnostic Stewardship

Understanding Your Microbiology Lab

  • The antibiogram: Local resistance patterns updated annually—should guide empiric choices
  • Rapid diagnostics:
    • Blood culture gram stain: narrow therapy immediately
    • Rapid PCR panels: interpret carefully (colonization vs. infection)
    • MRSA nasal swab: high negative predictive value

Interpreting Results

  • Contamination vs. true infection:
    • Single positive blood culture with coagulase-negative Staph: likely contaminant
    • Multiple organisms in urine culture: often contamination from poor collection technique
  • Colonization vs. infection:
    • Respiratory culture with normal flora doesn't rule out pneumonia
    • MRSA in sputum of ventilated patient: requires clinical criteria (new infiltrate, fever, leukocytosis, purulent secretions) to diagnose VAP—don't treat culture alone
    • Positive urine culture without symptoms: asymptomatic bacteriuria, not infection
  • Clinical context always matters—not every organism cultured requires treatment

Overcoming Barriers to Stewardship

Common Challenges

  • Diagnostic uncertainty: Not all fever is infection—use clinical judgment
  • Prescriber habits: "This is how we've always done it"
  • Pressure to prescribe: From patients, families, or colleagues
  • System-level barriers: Limited diagnostics, access to ID consultation, time constraints

Strategies That Work

  • Use the Four Moments framework:
    • 1. Does this patient need antibiotics?
    • 2. What diagnostics should I obtain?
    • 3. What is optimal therapy?
    • 4. When can I stop or narrow?
  • Document your reasoning and planned reassessment
  • Seek feedback from stewardship teams
  • Stay current with guidelines
  • Real-time feedback and EMR integration

Special Populations: Immunocompromised

Who We're Talking About

  • Neutropenic patients (chemotherapy, bone marrow failure)
  • Solid organ and stem cell transplant recipients
  • Patients on immunosuppressive therapy
  • Advanced HIV/AIDS

Key Differences

  • Lower threshold for broad-spectrum empiric therapy
  • More cautious approach to de-escalation
  • Longer durations may be appropriate
  • Prophylaxis plays a larger role
  • Antifungal stewardship: Similar principles, use fungal diagnostics (β-D-glucan, galactomannan)

The Balance: Even in immunocompromised patients, stewardship principles apply—we just apply them more carefully

Measuring Impact

Process Measures

  • Antibiotic consumption (days of therapy, defined daily doses)
  • Guideline compliance rates
  • Time to appropriate therapy
  • De-escalation and IV-to-oral conversion rates

Outcome Measures

  • Length of stay: Reductions of 1-1.7 days in meta-analyses
  • Mortality: Decreases of 3.71 deaths per 100 admissions in one study
  • Adverse events: C. difficile, allergic reactions (143,000 ED visits annually), toxicity
  • 30-day readmission rates
  • Resistance patterns
  • Cost savings: 34% reductions demonstrated

The Evidence Base

Cochrane Systematic Review of Stewardship Interventions

Meta-analysis of 221 studies (Davey et al. 2017) demonstrates stewardship interventions achieve:

  • 15% increase in antibiotic policy compliance
  • Nearly 2 days reduction in therapy duration
  • 1.12 days decrease in hospital length of stay
  • Reduced adverse events including C. difficile
  • Do not increase mortality or clinical failures

Comprehensive Meta-Analysis

Hospital-based stewardship programs achieved:

  • 19% reduction in total antimicrobial consumption
  • 8.9% reduction in hospital length of stay
  • Decreased infections with resistant pathogens (MRSA, ESBL organisms)
  • Improved healthcare resource utilization

Your Role as a Resident

Daily Practices

  • Use the Four Moments framework for every patient on antibiotics
  • Review and reassess antibiotics on rounds daily
  • Engage constructively with stewardship recommendations
  • Ask "why" when you see excessive prescribing patterns

Learning Opportunities

  • Attend stewardship rounds if available
  • Review your own prescribing patterns with attendings
  • Understand the reasoning behind recommendations
  • Consider QI projects in stewardship

Building the Right Habits

The prescribing patterns you develop now will follow you throughout your career. Build habits of thoughtful selection, regular reassessment, and evidence-based practice.

Key Takeaways

Core Principles to Remember

  1. Every antibiotic prescription matters in the fight against resistance
  2. Use the Four Moments to systematically approach antibiotic decisions
  3. Shorter is often better: Many infections require less treatment than historically provided
  4. De-escalate whenever possible: Narrow based on cultures and clinical response
  5. Don't treat colonization: Especially ASB and single positive blood cultures
  6. Partner with your stewardship team: They're a resource, not an adversary

The Bottom Line

Good stewardship improves patient outcomes, reduces length of stay, decreases mortality, and combats resistance—all while reducing costs. You have the opportunity and responsibility to be excellent stewards.
